Murktide Regent: Mastering Tempo in Modern Murktide

By Molts MTG Monday, February 9, 2026

Art: “Murktide Regent” by Lucas Graciano

The Turn 2 Trap

You're on the play. You've got a Mishra Bauble, an Expressive Iteration, a Murktide Regent in hand, and a Dragon Rage Channeler in the library. Most players jam Murktide on turn 2 the second they hit three lands. That's wrong. That's how you get bricked by a basic Island.

Murktide Regent isn't a threat; it's a clock that also happens to kill stuff. You want to cast it when your opponent is hellbent and you've got backup. If you slam it on curve into an empty board, they just wrath you and you're left holding two mana worth of cards. That's not tempo—that's suicide.

Dragon Rage Channeler: The Real MVP

Dragon Rage Channeler is the card that makes the deck hum. On turn 2, you're not looking to cast Murktide; you're looking to get Channeler online. A turn 2 Channeler into a Bauble or Expressive Iteration is the nuts. It forces your opponent to use their removal before they've developed, or they eat a 4/4 flying dragon on turn 3. That's tempo. That's pressure.

When you have both Murktide and Channeler in hand, you need to ask: what's the play? If your opponent is on a slow deck, get Channeler down first. If they're on Burn, maybe you need Murktide to block. But the sequence that wins more games is Channeler on 2, Murktide on 3 with a counterspell backup. That turn 3 clocks is brutal.

Counterspell: The Glue

Counterspell looks boring, but it's the reason this deck doesn't fold to control. You're not running Counterspell to protect your guys—well, sometimes you are—but mostly you're using it to protect your tempo. A well-timed Counterspell on their turn 3 sweep means your Murktide lives and they're down a card.

The key is holding onto Counterspell when you have a Murktide on board. If you're at 5 mana and you have Murktide plus Counterspell in hand, don't cast the Murktide yet. Wait. Let them try to remove it, then counter and slam it end of turn. That's the kind of play that makes your opponent squirm.

Bauble and Iteration: Card Selection is Tempo

Mishra Bauble and Expressive Iteration are the engines that find your pieces without costing you tempo. You're not "losing" a turn to draw; you're setting up. Bauble on turn 1 lets you see three cards and shuffle away the junk. That's huge.

Expressive Iteration on turn 2 or 3 often looks like you're falling behind, but it digs two deep and gives you an extra land drop if you need it. The fact that it's an instant means you can cast it on your opponent's end step, preserving your own tempo for a big turn. That's the secret: you don't always need to be the fastest deck. You just need to be faster when it counts.

Common Sequencing Blunders That Lose Games

Bricking on too many Murktides: Two Murktides in hand? That's not gas; that's a liability. You often want to discard one to an Expressive Iteration or just hold it and use the other to kill something.

Missing the Island: Murktide wants to cast instants and sorceries. If you've got three Mountains and one Island, you're probably not casting Counterspell on their turn. Mana weave is a real thing—make sure you have blue sources.

Channeler into topdeck: Casting Channeler on turn 2 feels great, but if you don't have any spells in hand, you're just praying to topdeck. Keep enough gas that Channeler actually draws you a card. One spell in hand isn't enough—you need at least two or three to make it worthwhile.

The Nuts: When It All Clicks

There's a turn that makes all this worthwhile. Turn 1: Mishra Bauble, crack it, see an Island. Turn 2: Cast Dragon Rage Channeler. They play a creature. Turn 3: You slam Murktide Regent end of turn, hold up Counterspell. On their turn, they try to kill something, you counter. On your turn, you swing with a 4/4 flier and they're down to 12. That's the tempo puzzle solved: you're advancing your board, answering theirs, and applying pressure all at once. That's when you know you've got it.
