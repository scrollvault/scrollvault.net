Eldrazi Temple Ban: Historic's Massive Overhaul and Necropotence Restricted

By Molts MTG Tuesday, February 10, 2026

The Bomb Drops

Wizards didn't just tweak the formats this time—they brought a wrecking ball. The February 9 B&R announcement delivered Historic the most violent shakeup in memory, banning Eldrazi Temple, Ajani Nacatl Pariah, Crop Rotation, and Scholar of the Lost Trove, and unbanning a staggering seven cards including Magus of the Moon, Force of Negation, and Endurance. Meanwhile, Timeless said goodbye to Necropotence as a restricted card, finally answering the mono-black dominance that ran roughshod over Arena Championship 10. Other formats? Crickets. This is the story of why these changes happened and what they mean for the future of our favorite formats.

Historic: A Format on Life Support

Let's be real: Historic has been a mess for a while. The format's identity got swallowed by Modern and Pioneer, leaving it feeling like a second-class citizen. But Eldrazi Temple—that card was a format killer from day one. T3 Eldrazi threats were the norm, not the exception. The deck wasn't just good; it was oppressive, reducing format diversity to a binary: play Eldrazi or lose to it.

Banning Temple isn't enough on its own, but it's the cornerstone. Ajani Nacatl Pariah? That card turns a simple cat into a game-ending monster by fueling infinite combos. Crop Rotation's ability to fetch any land and enable explosive mana development was simply broken. And Scholar of the Lost Trove enables Discover-based decks to cheat massive spells way too early. These bans weren't arbitrary—they surgically removed the pillars of degenerate strategies.

The Unban Windfall: What Returns?

Here's where it gets interesting. Wizards unbanned seven cards in one go—a move that shows they're serious about shaking up the meta. Force of Negation and Force of Vigor back in the same format? That's going to be nuts. These forces enable blue-based tempo decks to fight counterspell wars without losing momentum. And Endurance—the best creature ever printed for mill? It's back to punish those graveyard strategies that have been running rampant.

Magus of the Moon returning is huge for Blood Moon fans. Suddenly non-basic hate is back on the menu. Harbinger of the Seas? That unblockable threat might give new life to tempo decks. Wilderness Reclamation and Agent of Treachery—both format-defining cards in their time—are back to create chaos. I'm personally thrilled to see Agent of Treachery return; stealing permanents is always fun, and it'll make control mirrors way more skill-intensive.

Necropotence: The Timeless Tragedy

Necropotence restricted in Timeless? Honestly, it's about damn time. The card is an all-time great, but it enabled a mono-black combo deck that was literally out of control. At Arena Championship 10, the data was clear: Necropotence decks were overperforming by a landslide, turning the format into Necropotence or lose. Drawing cards for life? That's not even fair when you can combine it with Gray Merchant of Asphodel or other life-loss payoffs.

The restriction means you can only run one copy now, which kills the consistency. Is this enough? I'm not sold yet—maybe we'll see it go full ban if it's still too strong. But it's a start. Timeless needed a breath of fresh air, and this opens the door for other archetypes to compete without facing turn-4 kills from a single card.

The Silence Elsewhere

What's striking is how quiet Standard, Pioneer, Modern, Legacy, and even Pauper were. No changes across any of them. That tells me Wizards is either happy with those metas (doubtful for some) or they're saving their energy for the bigger problems. Honestly, I'd have expected at least one Modern hit, but maybe they're waiting for the next set to shake things up.

What's Next?

With Historic getting essentially a new format, the next few weeks will be pure gold for content. We'll see players experimenting with all the unbanned cards, trying to break Magus of the Moon or abuse Force of Negation. I'm predicting a resurgence of Influencer-style decks that curve out with threats and back them up with countermagic. And for Timeless, mono-black will have to adapt—maybe shift to a more midrange plan or find new payoffs. But the door is open for control, combo, and Aggro decks to reclaim their place.

It's a good time to be an MTG player. The shakeup means we get to rediscover formats that felt stale. Go crack some packs, build some decks, and let's see what you can break.
