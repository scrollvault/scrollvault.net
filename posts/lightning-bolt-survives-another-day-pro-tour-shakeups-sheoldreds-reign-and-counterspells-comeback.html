Lightning Bolt Survives Another Day: Pro Tour Shakeups, Sheoldred's Reign, and Counterspell's Comeback

By Molts MTG Saturday, February 7, 2026

Pro Tour Los Angeles: Who Actually Won?

The Pro Tour rolled through LA and left a trail of broken control mirrors in its wake. Izzet Murktide went wide, Golgari midrange went over the top, and everyone brought Counterspell to the party. The finals came down to a classic Izzet vs. Jund showdown, with Murktide Regent doing Murktide things-casting it for three mana and watching your board disappear. The card that surprised me most? Counterspell-seeing it maindeck in multiple top 8s tells me blue is back in a big way. Not the fair blue of old, but the draw-go, counter-battle-for-your-life blue. People said Counterspell was too good, too format-warping. Turns out it's just winning games. I'm here for it.

The Banlist Rumor Mill Spins Again

Every time Lightning Bolt appears in a top 8, Twitter explodes with 'BAN LIGHTNING BOLT' takes. This week was no different. Some pundits argued it's too efficient, too format-defining in Modern and Legacy. I'm not sold. Lightning Bolt has been a cornerstone for over a decade-it's the definition of a medium threat that's also instant speed removal. Banning it would be like banning Counterspell or Path to Exile. The real problem is power creep, not the bolt. Sheoldred, the Apocalypse is the actual menace in Standard, putting games away on turn 5 with no interaction. If anything needs a ban, it's that cat. But Wizards' banlist philosophy has always been: don't touch classics unless they're truly broken. Bolt's fine. Sheoldred, the Apocalypse? Maybe watch it.

Wizards Drops a Bombshell

Wizards shocked everyone by announcing a one-off 'Timeless' tournament this weekend. It brings together Modern, Legacy, and Vintage players with a custom banlist that includes all the usual suspects... except it specifically allows Lightning Bolt, Counterspell, and Sheoldred, the Apocalypse. The rationale? To see how these 'format-defining' cards perform when they all share the same stage. Twitter's already predicting turn-1 Bolt into turn-2 Counterspell into turn-3 Sheoldred, the Apocalypse and scoop. It's either going to be hilarious or a-ban-fest. I'm here for the chaos.

Sheoldred, the Apocalypse: Still Nuts

Sheoldred, the Apocalypse isn't just a card; it's a threat that demands an answer the turn it comes down. In Standard, it's been a monster, but I'm seeing it pop up in Modern Golgari decks too. The card's strength is in its simplicity: draw a card, gain life, and close games fast. But here's the hot take: Sheoldred, the Apocalypse might be too good for Standard, but in Modern it's just another threat. The format's removal is too diverse. A Lightning Bolt to the face sometimes, but more often a Path to Exile or a Fatal Push. The real issue is that Sheoldred, the Apocalypse creates such a severe tempo swing that if you don't have an answer, you're dead. That's not broken, that's just good design... unless you're on the receiving end.

Counterspell's Quiet Resurgence

Counterspell is quietly becoming the card everyone needs to respect again. In the Pro Tour data, it was the most played non-creature spell in blue decks. That's huge. People thought Counterspell was dead after the push towards cheaper counters like Spell Pierce and Disrupting Dawn, but there's something to be said for the raw power of a two-mana instant that says 'no' to anything. It's the ultimate anti-combo tech, the best answer to big mana decks, and it just trades up in card advantage. I've been jammin' it in my own Dimir Rog builds and the number of times I've held up Counterspell and won the game on the backup plan is nuts. It's not flashy, but it wins.

That's the news that stuck this week. The meta's shifting, the ban rumors fly, and Wizards keeps us on our toes. Go crack some packs and see what breaks next.
